If you're a PCBU under the Health and Safety at Work Act 2015, you have two
distinct jobs when something goes wrong: **notify** WorkSafe when an event is
notifiable, and **keep records** that show you're managing risks day to day.
Most businesses over-focus on the first and under-invest in the second — and
it's the second one that determines whether your everyday hazards get fixed
before they become notifiable events.

## What counts as notifiable

A notifiable event is a death, a notifiable injury or illness (the kind that
requires immediate treatment or hospitalisation), or a notifiable incident —
a near miss that exposed someone to a serious risk. These must be reported to
WorkSafe as soon as possible, and you must keep records of them for at least
five years.

## The part everyone forgets: the everyday record

The Act's real engine is the duty to manage risks so far as is reasonably
practicable — and you can't manage what nobody wrote down. A worker who
mentions a loose guard rail at smoko has given you knowledge; if there's no
system that captures it, assigns it, and closes it out, that knowledge
evaporates. If that rail later injures someone, the question isn't just "did
you notify?" — it's "what did you know, and what did you do about it?"

## What a defensible reporting habit looks like

- **Zero-friction capture.** If reporting takes longer than sending a text,
  workers won't do it. Photo plus one sentence should be enough.
- **Automatic routing.** Reports should land with the person who owns the
  fix, not in a folder someone checks monthly.
- **A status trail.** Spotted, in progress, resolved — with names and dates
  attached automatically.
- **A harder path for serious events.** Hazards and incidents need evidence,
  witness accounts and a root cause analysis before anyone can call them
  closed.

That last point matters: the discipline shouldn't depend on someone
remembering the process on the worst day of the month. Build it into the
tool, and the audit trail writes itself.
